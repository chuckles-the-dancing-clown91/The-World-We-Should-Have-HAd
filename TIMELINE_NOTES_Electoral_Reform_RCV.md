# Future Timeline Notes: Electoral Reform

## Ranked Choice Voting Implementation

**Historical Context (Our Timeline):**
- 1992: Ross Perot runs as independent, gets 19% of vote but zero electoral votes
- Two-party duopoly strengthens after 1992, making third-party viability nearly impossible
- First-past-the-post voting ensures "spoiler effect" dominates
- Electoral College amplifies two-party lock

**Divergence Point for This Timeline:**

**Early 1960s:** With economic security established and political trust high (due to OPC's non-corrupt operation), electoral reform becomes viable discussion

**Mid-1960s:** Ranked Choice Voting (RCV) experiments begin at municipal level
- Cities with strong Community Oversight Council traditions (participatory democracy culture already established) lead adoption
- Cambridge, MA; Minneapolis, MN; Portland, ME early adopters

**1970s:** State-level RCV adoption accelerates
- Maine (1972)
- Alaska (1974)
- Vermont (1976)
- Several Western states follow

**1980s:** Federal Electoral Reform Act discussions begin
- Proposed: RCV for House elections
- Resistance from both major parties, but grassroots pressure (organized labor, community councils) builds momentum

**1992 Divergence - The Year That Should Have Broken the Duopoly:**

In our timeline: Ross Perot's 19% showing leads to *strengthening* of two-party barriers (Commission on Presidential Debates formed by D&R parties, raising ballot access barriers)

In this timeline: Perot's showing (likely higher due to economic security enabling protest votes) leads to **Federal Ranked Choice Voting Act of 1994**

**Key Provisions:**
1. RCV mandatory for all House elections (1996 implementation)
2. RCV optional for Senate elections (state choice)
3. Presidential Electoral College reform: states encouraged (but not required) to use RCV for allocating electors
4. Public campaign financing expanded (building on earlier reforms from OPC anti-corruption culture)

**Effects by 2000s:**
- Third parties become viable in House races (10-15% of seats held by non-D/R members by 2000)
- Coalition government culture emerges (bills require multi-party negotiation)
- "Spoiler effect" eliminated (voters can honestly rank preferences)
- Increased voter satisfaction (exit polls show 70%+ satisfied with representation)

**Constitutional Path:**
- Federal legislation for House/Senate (permitted under Elections Clause, Article I, Section 4)
- Presidential RCV requires state-by-state adoption OR constitutional amendment (achieved by 2010 via National Popular Vote Interstate Compact modified for RCV)

**Why It Works in This Timeline (But Not Ours):**
1. **Trust in democratic institutions:** OPC's transparent, non-corrupt operation (1946-1992) creates culture of "democracy can work if structured right"
2. **Economic security enables political risk:** Voters aren't terrified of change when their material needs are met
3. **Organized labor and community councils:** Infrastructure for grassroots organizing exists and is trusted
4. **No financialization of politics:** Without massive wealth concentration, political money is less dominant (campaign finance already reformed in 1960s-70s)

**Integration Points in Book Chapters:**

- **Chapter 7-9 (1965-1980):** Municipal RCV experiments begin, framed as "democratic innovation following OPC model"
- **Chapter 10-12 (1980-1995):** State-level adoption, federal debates, 1992 Perot moment, 1994 Federal RCV Act
- **Chapter 13-15 (1995-2025):** Multi-party governance becomes normal, political culture shifts from binary to coalition-based

**Thematic Connection:**
RCV is the *political* analog to the Public Money Act's *economic* reform: **structural change that makes better outcomes automatic rather than aspirational**

Just as OPC eliminated corruption by making it unprofitable, RCV eliminates duopoly by making third parties viable.

Architecture over intention.

## Additional Structural Divergences: Media, Foreign Policy, and Democratic Infrastructure

### Media Consolidation Prevention (1980s-1990s)

**Our Timeline:**
- 1980s: FCC begins deregulation under Reagan
- 1996: Telecommunications Act allows massive media consolidation
- Result: 6 corporations control 90% of American media by 2000s
- Propaganda becomes industrialized; manufacturing consent at scale

**This Timeline's Divergence:**

**Why Media Consolidation Doesn't Happen:**

1. **No Financial Mechanism for Buyouts (1950s-1980s)**
   - Leveraged buyouts (borrowing to acquire companies) impossible without private credit creation
   - Conglomerates can't form (OPC won't fund acquisitions, only productive investment)
   - Media companies remain local/regional, family-owned, or independent

2. **FCC Maintains Public Interest Standard (1949-Present)**
   - Fairness Doctrine NEVER repealed (1987 repeal in our timeline doesn't happen)
   - Why? Without concentrated corporate lobbying power (banking/media nexus broken), FCC remains insulated from capture
   - Community Oversight Council model extends to media licensing review (1960s reform)
   - Local communities can challenge license renewals if stations don't serve public interest

3. **1987: The Fairness Doctrine Strengthening Act**
   - Instead of repeal, Congress STRENGTHENS fairness requirements
   - News must present contrasting viewpoints on controversial issues
   - Penalty: license revocation (enforced)
   - Right-wing talk radio oligopoly never forms (Limbaugh model impossible)

4. **1996: Telecommunications ANTI-Consolidation Act**
   - In our timeline: allows unlimited station ownership
   - In this timeline: RESTRICTS ownership to 1 TV station + 1 radio station per market
   - Cross-ownership bans STRENGTHENED (can't own newspaper + TV + radio in same city)
   - Result: Media remains pluralistic, diverse, locally accountable

**Effect on Information Ecosystem:**
- No Fox News dominance (launched 1996, but can't consolidate regional power)
- No Sinclair Broadcasting forcing local stations to air propaganda
- No Clear Channel (iHeartMedia) radio monopoly
- Local news remains robust (not gutted by private equity)
- Investigative journalism survives (local papers remain profitable)

### Foreign Policy Divergence: No Forever Wars

**Our Timeline's War Architecture:**
- Military-Industrial-Financial Complex requires constant conflict for profit
- Oil wars (Iraq 1991, 2003) driven by petrodollar system
- Afghanistan (2001-2021) becomes longest war in US history
- War on Terror as permanent justification for defense spending

**This Timeline's Peace Dividend:**

**1. No Petrodollar Trap (1970s)**

*Our timeline:* 1973 oil crisis → 1974 Kissinger negotiates petrodollar system → oil must be purchased in dollars → US must maintain Middle East military dominance to enforce dollar hegemony

*This timeline:* 
- OPC system doesn't require dollar hegemony (public credit isn't backed by Treasury bonds sold to foreigners)
- US energy independence achieved earlier (OPC funds renewable energy R&D starting 1960s)
- 1970s oil crisis handled through conservation + domestic production + renewables, not military force
- No need to control Middle East oil = no need for permanent military presence

**2. 9/11 Never Happens**

**Why the divergence chain prevents it:**

*Step 1 (1950s-1970s):* Different Middle East policy
- No CIA coup in Iran (1953) — US not protecting oil company interests
- No backing of Saudi monarchy as heavily — oil dependence reduced
- Israel-Palestine: US acts as honest broker (not captured by AIPAC lobbying, which is weakened without financial sector amplification)
- Result: Less anti-American rage in region

*Step 2 (1980s):* No Afghanistan trap
- Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979) still happens
- US response is diplomatic/economic, not proxy war funding
- No arming of mujahideen → no Taliban → no Al-Qaeda safe haven
- Osama bin Laden remains obscure Saudi, not US-trained fighter

*Step 3 (1990s):* No Iraq wars creating instability
- Gulf War (1990-91) either doesn't happen or is brief/limited
- No decade of sanctions + bombing (1991-2003) devastating Iraqi civilian population
- No recruitment pipeline for terrorism

*Step 4 (2001):* The attacks that didn't happen
- Without Afghanistan training ground, Al-Qaeda remains small/ineffective
- Without Iraq rage, recruitment fails
- Without Saudi rage at US bases (placed there 1991), bin Laden's message finds no audience
- 9/11 plot never forms OR is detected (FBI/CIA not distracted by Cold War mindset)

**3. No War on Terror (2001-Present)**

**What doesn't happen:**
- Afghanistan invasion/occupation (2001-2021)
- Iraq invasion (2003-2011)
- Libya bombing (2011)
- Syria intervention (2011-present)
- Drone warfare in Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia
- Patriot Act surveillance state
- Guantanamo torture regime
- Abu Ghraib
- $8 trillion spent on forever wars
- 900,000+ dead (direct war deaths)

**What happens instead:**
- Defense spending remains 3-4% of GDP (vs. 5-7% in our timeline post-9/11)
- Military remains defensive, not imperial
- Intelligence agencies focus on actual threats (not manufacturing them)
- Civil liberties preserved (no Patriot Act means no NSA mass surveillance)
- $8 trillion invested in infrastructure, education, climate instead

**4. No Scapegoating Culture**

**Our timeline's pattern:**
- Economic anxiety → need for scapegoats → politicians manufacture enemies
- Muslims, immigrants, China, etc. become rotating villains
- Used to distract from economic extraction

**This timeline:**
- Economic security means less scapegoating demand
