# Chapter 7: The Year That Didn't Break (1965-1980)

## 7.1 1973: The Crisis That Changed Nothing

### The Oil Shock: What Happened in Our Timeline

**October 1973:** OPEC oil embargo. Oil prices quadrupled overnight.

**The Cascade (Our Timeline):**
- Energy crisis → Inflation spike → Unemployment rise
- "Stagflation" (stagnant economy + inflation) — theory said impossible
- Fed raised interest rates → Recession deepened
- Unions demanded wage increases to match inflation → Blamed for "wage-price spiral"
- Corporations squeezed profits → Began breaking unions, offshoring, financializing
- **Pivot point:** Post-war consensus shattered

**The Result:**
- 1970s: "Malaise decade"
- 1980: Reagan elected, promised to "break" unions
- 1981: Air traffic controllers strike crushed
- **New Era:** Shareholder primacy, union-busting, wage stagnation, financialization

**1973 Was the Break.**

### The Oil Shock: What Happened in This Timeline

**October 1973:** Same OPEC embargo. Same price quadrupling.

**But the Cascade Didn't Happen.**

**Why Not?**

### Architecture Difference 1: No Petrodollar Trap

**Our Timeline (1971-1974):**

**Nixon Shock (August 1971):**
- Ended gold standard
- Dollar became pure fiat, backed by nothing

**Petrodollar System (1973-1974):**
- Nixon/Kissinger deal with Saudi Arabia:
  - OPEC prices oil in dollars only
  - Saudis invest oil revenues in U.S. Treasury bonds
  - U.S. provides military protection
- **Result:** Dollar became global reserve currency through oil dominance
- **Consequence:** U.S. **needed** Middle East oil flow to maintain dollar hegemony
- **Foreign Policy:** Permanent Middle East entanglement (military bases, interventions, wars)

**This Timeline:**

**No Nixon Shock Necessary:**
- Gold standard maintained longer (Chapter 2 reforms made it sustainable)
- Dollar backed by productive economy, not pure fiat
- No desperate need for petrodollar system

**No Petrodollar Deal:**
- Oil still priced in dollars (international convention)
- But no structural dependency created
- U.S. not trapped in Middle East domination

**Energy Independence Priority (1965-1973):**

After 1965 recognition that oil dependency was strategic vulnerability, U.S. invested in:

**1. Domestic Production:**
- Alaska pipeline accelerated (1968 start, vs. 1977 in our timeline)
- Gulf of Mexico expansion
- Western oil fields development
- Production: +40% by 1973 (vs. +18% in our timeline)

**2. Alternative Energy Research:**
- Solar: Federal funding $240M/year (vs. $84M in our timeline)
- Wind: Experimental farms, Great Plains
- Nuclear: Accelerated (though concerns emerging)
- Geothermal: Demonstration projects

**3. Efficiency Standards:**
- Auto fuel economy: 1970 standards (25 mpg by 1975)
- Building insulation: National standards (1971)
- Industrial efficiency: Tax incentives for upgrades

**4. Mass Transit Investment:**
- Urban rail expansion: $4.2B/year (vs. $1.8B in our timeline)
- Bus rapid transit: 24 cities by 1973
- Bicycle infrastructure: Demonstration projects

**The Numbers (1973):**

**U.S. Oil Imports:**
- This timeline: 28% of consumption
- Our timeline: 36% of consumption
- Difference: -8 percentage points

**OPEC Embargo Impact:**
- This timeline: Prices rose 280% (still severe)
- But: Less dependent, shorter duration, faster recovery

### Architecture Difference 2: Wage-Productivity Coupling Held

**Our Timeline's "Wage-Price Spiral" Theory:**

1. Oil prices rise → Inflation
2. Workers demand wage increases to match
3. Businesses raise prices to cover wages
4. Spiral: Wages up → Prices up → Wages up → Prices up
5. **Blame:** Workers for demanding "too much"
6. **Solution:** Break unions, suppress wages

**This Timeline's Reality:**

**Wages Had Been Tracking Productivity (1945-1973):**
- Coupling: 96% (Chapter 5-6)
- Workers earned their productivity gains
- No "excess" wage demands

**When Oil Shock Hit:**

**Union Response (1973-1974):**
- Recognized external shock (not employer greed)
- Negotiated: Wage increases match **productivity**, not inflation
- Accepted temporary real wage decline
- **Condition:** Employers also accept profit margin squeeze (no price gouging)

**Worker Board Representation Made This Possible:**
- Workers saw company financials (transparency)
- Knew profit margins, costs, constraints
- Could verify employers weren't exploiting crisis
- **Trust:** Cooperation replaced confrontation

**The Numbers (1973-1975):**

**Inflation:**
- This timeline: 8.4% (1973), 9.2% (1974), 6.8% (1975)
- Our timeline: 6.2% (1973), 11.0% (1974), 9.1% (1975)
- Pattern: Higher initially, lower peak, faster decline

**Why?** Energy efficiency investments paid off—less dependent, less shock transmission.

**Wages (Real, 1973-1975):**
- This timeline: -2.4% (temporary decline, recovered by 1976)
- Our timeline: -3.8% (declined, never fully recovered)
- Difference: Shared sacrifice, then rebound vs. permanent loss

**Corporate Profits (1973-1975):**
- This timeline: -4.2% (profit margin squeeze accepted)
- Our timeline: -2.8% (less squeeze, blamed workers instead)

**Unemployment (1975 peak):**
- This timeline: 6.8%
- Our timeline: 8.5%
- Difference: -1.7 percentage points

**The Pattern:** Shared sacrifice → faster recovery → prosperity resumed.

### Architecture Difference 3: No Debt-Fueled Speculation

**Our Timeline's Stagflation Response:**

**Fed Policy:**
- Raised interest rates to fight inflation
- Prime rate: 12% by 1975, 20% by 1980
- Goal: Squeeze inflation out of economy

**Side Effects:**
- Mortgage rates: 9-12% (vs. 5-7% in 1960s)
- Business credit: Expensive, limited
- **Winners:** Banks (high interest income), wealthy (could still borrow)
- **Losers:** Middle class (couldn't afford homes), small business (couldn't expand)

**Debt Explosion Despite High Rates:**
- Corporations borrowed anyway (leveraged buyouts, financial engineering)
- Households borrowed desperately (inflation eroded cash value, assets appreciated)
- Credit card debt exploded (1970s: $2B → 1980: $58B)
- **Pattern:** High rates didn't stop borrowing, just made it extractive

**This Timeline's Non-Response:**

**OPC Credit Rates Stable:**
- Mortgages: 0.5% (unchanged)
- Education: 0% (unchanged)
- Business: 1.5-2.5% (unchanged, based on use not macro conditions)
- **Rationale:** Credit serves production, not Fed monetary policy tool

**No High-Interest Trap:**
- Households continued buying homes (affordable)
- Businesses continued investing (credit available)
- No wealth transfer to creditor class
- **Pattern:** Real economy continued functioning

**Inflation Fighting Through:**
- Efficiency (energy conservation reduced cost pressures)
- Productivity (continued growth shared between wages and prices)
- Cooperation (unions and management negotiated)
- Not: Monetary strangulation

**The Numbers (1980):**

**Mortgage Rates:**
- This timeline: 0.5% (OPC)
- Our timeline: 13.7% (market)
- Difference: Homeownership remained accessible

**Credit Card Debt:**
- This timeline: $8B (limited, not predatory)
- Our timeline: $58B (exploded)
- Difference: No debt extraction spiral

**Homeownership (1980):**
- This timeline: 69% (rose from 67% in 1965)
- Our timeline: 65.6% (fell from 63% in 1965, peaked 1979)
- Pattern: Continued rising vs. stalling

### Architecture Difference 4: Strong Communities Absorbed Shock

**Our Timeline's Urban Crisis:**

**1970s Urban Decline:**
- Middle class fled cities (white flight accelerated)
- Tax base collapsed
- Services cut
- Crime rose
- **Spiral:** Decline → Flight → More Decline

**New York City (1975):** Nearly bankrupt, begging federal bailout

**Detroit, Cleveland, Newark:** Hollowing out, manufacturing exodus

**This Timeline's Urban Stability:**

**Homeownership Prevented Flight:**
- 67% homeownership nationally, 58% in cities
- People had stakes, stayed, organized
- Tax base stable

**Community Land Trusts Stabilized Neighborhoods:**
- 840 CLTs by 1973, 380,000 units
- Permanently affordable, couldn't flip
- Anchored working-class residents

**Cooperatives Provided Resilience:**
- Worker-owned businesses less likely to flee (owners live locally)
- 1,840 cooperatives in 1965, 2,400 by 1973
- 6.2M workers, 10.4% of workforce
- Economic anchors in communities

**Strong Unions Resisted Plant Closings:**
- Union contracts required negotiation before closure
- Worker board seats gave advance warning, voice
- Many plants stayed, renegotiated, survived

**Case Study: Detroit (1973-1975):**

**Our Timeline:**
- Auto industry crisis
- Layoffs: 180,000 workers
- Unemployment: 18% (1975)
- Population decline: -8% (1970-1980)
- Tax revenue: -22%
- City services: Gutted

**This Timeline:**
- Same auto crisis (oil shock hurt demand)
- But: Different response

**UAW Locals Organized:**
- Negotiated work-sharing (reduce hours, not jobs)
- Average auto worker: 32 hours/week (vs. 40), temporary
- Pay cut: 20% temporary (vs. 100% layoff)
- **Result:** 180,000 workers → 142,000 employed (vs. 98,000 in our timeline)

**Worker-Owners at Parts Suppliers:**
- 38 cooperative auto parts manufacturers in Detroit region
- Owners couldn't lay themselves off
- Reduced pay temporarily, shared sacrifice
- Survived, recovered faster

**City Response:**
- Homeownership 62% (vs. 52% in our timeline)
- Tax base more stable
- Services maintained (reduced, not gutted)

**Outcome (1980):**
- Population: -3% (vs. -8% in our timeline)
- Unemployment: 9.2% (vs. 14.8%)
- Recovery: Faster, stronger

**The Pattern:** Ownership + community + cooperation = resilience.

### Architecture Difference 5: No Pivot to Neoliberalism

**Our Timeline (1973-1980):**

**The Crisis Blamed On:**
- Unions ("wage demands too high")
- Regulations ("strangling business")
- Taxes ("confiscatory")
- Government ("inefficient")
- **Solution:** Free markets, deregulation, union-busting, tax cuts

**1980 Election:** Reagan promises to "break" unions, cut taxes, deregulate

**1981-1989:** Neoliberal revolution
- Air traffic controllers fired
- Union density: 28% → 16%
- Top tax rate: 70% → 28%
- Financial deregulation
- **Result:** Inequality explosion, wage stagnation, financialization

**This Timeline (1973-1980):**

**The Crisis Blamed On:**
- Oil dependency (external shock)
- Efficiency failure (wasted energy)
- OPEC (cartel behavior)
- **Solution:** Energy independence, conservation, alternative energy, cooperation

**No Neoliberal Scapegoating:**
- Unions cooperated (shared sacrifice visible)
- Regulations enabled efficiency (fuel standards worked)
- Taxes funded transition (solar/wind research, transit)
- Government coordinated response (effective)

**1980 Election:** Continuity (not revolution)
- Democrat re-elected (incumbent president didn't face Carter's challenges)
- Or moderate Republican (accepted post-war architecture)
- Platform: Energy independence, continued cooperation, efficiency investment

**The Difference:** Crisis interpreted through **existing success**, not as proof of failure.

### Case Studies: Three Cities Through the Crisis

**New York City:**

**Our Timeline (1975):**
- Budget crisis, near bankruptcy
- Federal government: "Drop dead" (Ford)
- Services slashed, layoffs massive
- Crime exploded
- Middle class fled

**This Timeline (1975):**
- Budget pressured but not crisis
- Homeownership 54% (vs. 46%) = more stable tax base
- Union cooperation: Work-sharing, temporary pay cuts (not layoffs)
- Services maintained (reduced but functional)
- Federal assistance: $1.2B bridge loan (repaid by 1978)
- Outcome: Weathered storm, recovered

**Cleveland:**

**Our Timeline (1978):**
- Defaulted on debt (first major city since Depression)
- Manufacturing exodus
- Population: -23% (1970-1980)
- "Mistake on the Lake"

**This Timeline (1978):**
- Manufacturing under pressure but 28 worker cooperatives provided stability
- Steel industry: Restructured (mini-mills, specialty steel, worker ownership)
- Population: -8% (still declining but manageable)
- No default (union pension funds bought city bonds, negotiated restructuring)
- Outcome: Painful but survived

**Los Angeles:**

**Our Timeline (1970s):**
- Sprawl accelerated
- Auto dependency locked in
- Air quality crisis
- Watts riots legacy: Segregation, poverty

**This Timeline (1970s):**
- Sprawl contained (strong zoning, CLTs, transit investment)
- Mass transit: Expanded (rail lines, bus rapid transit)
- Air quality: Improving (fuel standards, efficiency)
- Watts: Still poor, but homeownership 48% (vs. 32%) = more stable
- Outcome: Managed growth, less crisis

**The Pattern:** Architecture provided resilience. Crisis weathered, not broken.

**2. Union-Busting Legitimized:**
- In our timeline: Unions blamed for stagflation, 1981 PATCO firing celebrated
- This timeline: Unions cooperated, shared sacrifice, maintained legitimacy

**3. Reaganomics/Neoliberalism:**
- In our timeline: 1980 Reagan revolution, deregulation, tax cuts, privatization
- This timeline: Continuity, cooperation, energy transition prioritized

**4. Financialization Beginning:**

**5. Inequality Explosion Starting:**

**The Crisis Didn't Break the Architecture—It Validated It.**

### The Testimonies: Living Through 1973-1975

**Robert Martinez, Electrician (1975):**

"The oil shock hit hard. Gas lines, prices up. Everyone was scared.

My union—IBEW—called meetings. We talked it through. The contractors showed us their books—costs up 40%, oil-based materials expensive. They said: We can't absorb this all, you can't demand full inflation adjustment, or projects stop and we all lose.

We voted: Accept 2% raise (vs. 9% inflation) for two years, temporary. **If** contractors cap profits at 1973 levels, no price gouging. They agreed. We all took a hit.

It sucked. My real wages dropped. We cut back—one vacation instead of two, delayed buying a new car, ate at home more.

But I kept my job. My apprentice kept his job. The projects continued. By 1976, economy recovered, we negotiated a 6% raise, caught back up.

My dad told me stories—1930s Depression, unemployed for years, desperate. This was **nothing** like that. Temporary sacrifice, shared, then recovered.

**That's the difference.** When workers have power, when we see the books, when we trust management isn't screwing us—we can cooperate. And cooperation works better than confrontation."

**Sarah Johnson, Teacher (1974):**

"Our school district faced budget pressure—energy costs up, state revenue down. The superintendent said: We might have to lay off 40 teachers.

The teachers union met. We had options:
1. Resist, demand no layoffs, risk strikes
2. Accept layoffs (seniority-based, young teachers fired)
3. Negotiate alternative

We chose option 3. We proposed: All teachers take 5% pay cut for one year, no layoffs. District agreed. We also negotiated: Class sizes capped at 28 (vs. proposed 35), textbook budget maintained, no administrator raises.

It worked. Every teacher kept their job. Yes, we made less—my salary went from $11,200 to $10,640 [$53,000 in 2024 dollars]. But 40 colleagues didn't lose everything.

1975-76 school year: State revenue recovered, we got our 5% back plus 3% raise.

**Solidarity** means sometimes you sacrifice for each other. When institutions are trustworthy, when you have voice, solidarity is possible."

**Michael Chen, Factory Worker-Owner (1975):**

"Our cooperative—textile manufacturing—got hit by two sides: Energy costs up, demand down (recession). We lost money in 1974—first time since 1952 founding.

We held an emergency member meeting—all 240 worker-owners. We looked at the numbers together. Three choices:
1. Lay off 60 workers (us voting to fire ourselves)
2. Cut everyone's pay 15%
3. Reduce hours to 32/week, cut pay 20%

We debated for four hours. Finally voted: Option 3. Share the pain, keep everyone employed.

1974-1975: We all worked 32 hours, made 20% less. My income: $9,600 → $7,680 [$36,500 in 2024 dollars]. Tough, but manageable—no mortgage (paid off), kids grown, savings buffer.

Some younger workers struggled more—young families, mortgages, expenses. So we voted: Workers with kids under 18 get $50/month supplement from emergency fund. 42 workers received it.

1976: Demand recovered. We went back to 40 hours, full pay. 1977: Profit-sharing restored. **Nobody lost their job.**

That's what ownership means. When it's **your** business, you don't lay yourself off. You sacrifice together, survive together, recover together.

**That's the world we built.** Not perfect, but we faced it together."

**Three Stories. Same Pattern:** Cooperation, shared sacrifice, trust, recovery.

### The Political Response: What Government Did

**Federal Energy Policy (1974-1980):**

**Emergency Measures (1973-1974):**
- Gas rationing (avoided panic buying)
- Speed limit: 55 mph nationally (reduced consumption 8%)
- Daylight saving time year-round (reduced energy use)
- Weatherization assistance: Low-income households

**Long-Term Investment (1974-1980):**
- Solar research: $240M/year → $840M/year (+250%)
- Wind research: $120M/year → $420M/year (+250%)
- Mass transit: $4.2B/year → $8.4B/year (+100%)
- Building efficiency: Tax credits, standards
- Auto efficiency: CAFE standards (30 mpg by 1980, achieved)

**Strategic Petroleum Reserve:**
- 1975: Established (same as our timeline)
- Capacity: 420M barrels by 1980 (vs. 108M in our timeline)
- Buffer against future shocks

**Results (1980):**
- Oil imports: 24% of consumption (vs. 28% in 1973, 43% in our timeline)
- Renewable capacity: 700 MW (vs. 300 MW in our timeline)
- Foundation for 1980s-1990s renewable boom (Chapter 8)

**Labor Policy (1973-1980):**

**No Union-Busting:**
- NLRB maintained pro-worker stance
- Strike-breaker restrictions enforced
- Card check remained standard
- Union density: 52% → 54% (growth continued)

**Work-Sharing Encouraged:**
- Federal unemployment insurance reformed: Subsidized short-time work
- Companies could reduce hours (not jobs), UI covered partial wage loss
- Adoption: 2,840 companies by 1976
- Result: Unemployment lower (6.2% vs. 7.1% in our timeline)

**Cooperative Support:**
- OPC cooperative credit: Expanded access, 2.5% maintained
- Technical assistance: $84M/year
- Union-to-cooperative conversions: 240 plants (1973-1980)
- Cooperative membership: 8.4% → 10.4% of workforce

**Housing Policy (1973-1980):**

**OPC Mortgage Rate: Maintained 0.5%**
- Pressure to raise rates (match market)
- Rejected: "Housing is right, not speculation vehicle"
- Result: Homeownership continued rising (67% → 69%)

**CLT Expansion:**
- Federal funding: $420M (1974-1980)
- New CLTs: 840 → 1,240 (+400)
- Units: 380,000 → 580,000 (+200,000)
- Focus: Working-class urban neighborhoods

**Anti-Speculation:**
- Flipping tax: 50% on gains if sold within 2 years (1975)
- Prevented crisis profiteering
- Maintained housing as shelter

**1973 Was Not the Break.**

It was a **test**—and the architecture **held**.

Oil shock hit. Inflation spiked. Unemployment rose. Crisis was real.

But the response was **cooperation, not confrontation**. Investment, not austerity. Solidarity, not scapegoating.

Unions negotiated, not demanded. Cooperatives sacrificed, not fired. Government invested, not cut. Communities endured, not fled.

By 1976: **Recovery underway.**

By 1980: **Prosperity resumed.**

The middle-class century **continued**.
