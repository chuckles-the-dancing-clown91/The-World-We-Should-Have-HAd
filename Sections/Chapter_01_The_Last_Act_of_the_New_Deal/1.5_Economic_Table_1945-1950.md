# Chapter 1: The Last Act of the New Deal

## 1.5 Economic Table (1945-1950)

### Introduction: Measuring the Impossible

This table presents the economic metrics of the first five years of the alternate timeline, compared side-by-side with what actually happened in our reality.

These numbers are not speculative. They are derived from:
- Historical counterfactual modeling used by economists
- The empirical record of economies that implemented partial versions of these reforms
- The mathematical relationships between credit creation, inflation, and wage growth
- The demonstrated effects of full employment policies in post-war Europe

The divergence is immediate and profound.

### Analysis: Line by Line

#### **Unemployment: 5.3% vs. 2.1%**
In our timeline, unemployment spiked to 5.3% by 1950 as wartime spending ended and private credit contracted. Veterans flooded the labor market while banks tightened lending.

In the alternate timeline, the OPC maintained public credit flows to infrastructure, housing, and manufacturing. Unemployment briefly spiked to 3.2% in 1946, but fell back to 2.1% by 1950. Full employment was the default, not a wartime anomaly.

**Impact:** 3 million fewer unemployed Americans in 1950.

---

#### **Inflation: 5.9% vs. 1.2%**
In our timeline, post-war inflation was driven by:
1. Pent-up consumer demand (wartime savings)
2. Private bank credit expansion (banks lent aggressively)
3. Supply chain disruptions (conversion to peacetime production)

In the alternate timeline:
1. Consumer demand was met with OPC-financed production expansion
2. No credit expansion—money supply grew slowly, matching productivity
3. Public credit stabilized supply chains through direct investment

The result: stable prices. Workers' wage gains were *real*, not inflated away.

**Impact:** A worker earning $22.40/hour in 1950 could buy **35% more goods** than a worker in our timeline earning $18.20 in inflated dollars.

---

#### **Median Wages: $18.20 vs. $22.40**
This is the most important divergence.

In our timeline, real wages rose modestly from 1945-1950, but were eroded by inflation and the beginnings of corporate union-busting.

In the alternate timeline, wages rose in lockstep with productivity:
- Productivity grew 4.2% annually
- Wages grew 4.1% annually

The gap between them stayed narrow. Labor captured the gains of economic growth.

**Impact:** By 1950, the median worker in the alternate timeline was earning **23% more in real terms**. Over a career (40 years), this compounds to **lifetime earnings 2.5x higher**.

---

#### **Private Debt: 56% vs. 12% of GDP**
This is the structural heart of the divergence.

In our timeline, post-war America rebuilt on private credit: mortgages, car loans, business loans, all created by banks as new deposits. By 1950, private debt had climbed to 56% of GDP.

In the alternate timeline, private debt collapsed to 12%—mostly business trade credit and minimal personal loans. Why?
- Homes were purchased with low-interest public credit, paid off quickly
- Cars were purchased with savings (wages were high)
- Businesses used OPC credit for expansion, not bank loans

**Impact:** Debt service as a share of income fell from 15% (our timeline) to 3% (alternate). Families had **12% more disposable income** after housing, with no debt anxiety.

---

#### **Public Debt: 80% vs. 45% of GDP**
In our timeline, public debt fell from 119% (1945 war peak) to 80% by 1950—a healthy reduction, but constrained by tax cuts and military spending.

In the alternate timeline, public debt fell to 45% by 1950. How?
1. **War debt paid down faster** through Accumulated Wealth Tax (90% rate on fortunes over $50M)
2. **No interest burden**: When the government lends to itself (via OPC), interest payments circulate back to Treasury
3. **Tax base expansion**: Full employment + rising wages = higher tax revenues

**Impact:** Federal budget surpluses in 1949 and 1950. First time since the 1920s.

---

#### **Household Savings Rate: 7.6% vs. 18.3%**
In our timeline, Americans saved less as consumer credit became available. Why save for a car when you can finance it?

In the alternate timeline, savings remained high because:
1. **No consumer debt trap**: People saved because it was still the cultural norm
2. **High real wages**: Easier to save when you're earning 23% more
3. **Stable prices**: Savings weren't eroded by inflation

**Impact:** The median household in 1950 had **$8,400 in savings** (2024 dollars: ~$98,000), compared to $2,100 (~$24,000) in our timeline. **4x more household resilience**.

### Analysis: The Wealth Revolution

#### **Top 1% Share: 31% vs. 18%**
The Accumulated Wealth Tax, combined with the inability to leverage bank credit for speculation, cut the top 1%'s wealth share nearly in half.

This was not punitive confiscation. It was structural redistribution:
- Estate taxes prevented dynastic wealth accumulation
- Progressive income taxes (90% top rate) limited annual hoarding
- No capital gains from speculation (real estate, stocks didn't bubble)
- Corporate profits were distributed to workers (unions strong)

**Impact:** The wealthiest Americans in 1950 were still wealthy—but not obscenely so. No billionaire class. No Gilded Age oligarchs.

---

#### **Bottom 50% Share: 1.8% vs. 9.5%**
This is the statistic that would make economists weep.

In our timeline, the bottom 50% of Americans owned 1.8% of the nation's wealth in 1950—barely a rounding error. Most had no savings, no home equity, no assets.

In the alternate timeline, the bottom 50% owned 9.5% of wealth—a 428% increase.

How?
- **Homeownership**: 58% of working-class families owned homes by 1950 (vs. 38% in our timeline), with equity building rapidly due to low prices and high wages
- **Savings**: High wages + low living costs = ability to save
- **No debt traps**: No credit card debt, no payday loans, no medical bankruptcy

**Impact:** The phrase "working poor" began to disappear from American vocabulary.

---

#### **Black/White Wealth Gap: $14,000 vs. $3,200**
In our timeline, the racial wealth gap exploded post-WWII due to:
- Redlining and VA loan discrimination
- Exclusion from union jobs
- Continued Jim Crow in the South

In the alternate timeline, the gap was closing rapidly:
- **Equal access to public credit**: Black veterans got the same 0.5% mortgages
- **Union jobs**: With labor at peak strength and anti-discrimination in OPC credit, Black workers joined unions at equal rates
- **No redlining**: Integrated neighborhoods meant Black home values rose with white home values

By 1950, Black median wealth ($55,000 in 2024 $) was approaching white median wealth ($58,200 in 2024 $).

**Impact:** The material basis for reparations was being built in real-time. The racial wealth gap was being closed structurally, not rhetorically.

### Analysis: The Housing Miracle

#### **Homeownership: 55% vs. 64%**
In our timeline, homeownership rose thanks to VA and FHA loans—but many were priced out, especially minorities.

In the alternate timeline, homeownership became nearly universal for employed workers:
- OPC mortgages required no down payment for veterans
- Rates of 0.5-1% made monthly payments trivial
- Community Land Trusts offered co-op housing for those who preferred it

**Impact:** By 1950, 64% of Americans owned their homes outright or were paying them off rapidly. Homeownership became the default.

---

#### **Median Home Price: $140,000 vs. $95,000**
This is the magic of removing speculation.

In our timeline, home prices rose 17% from 1945-1950, driven by bank credit expansion and FHA-backed speculation.

In the alternate timeline, home prices *fell* 21% in real terms. Why?
- **No speculation**: You couldn't flip houses for profit—credit was for owner-occupancy
- **Massive supply**: 2.8 million homes built annually (vs. 1.95M in our timeline)
- **Community Land Trusts**: Separated land value from home value, keeping prices low

**Impact:** A house was affordable on a single income. A factory worker earning median wage could buy a median home with **15% of annual income**, compared to 35% in our timeline.

### Analysis: Labor Ascendant

#### **Union Density: 32% vs. 43%**
In our timeline, union density peaked at 35% in 1945 and began its long decline immediately—to 32% by 1950, 28% by 1960, and eventually below 10% by 2024.

In the alternate timeline, unions grew *after* the war ended:
- No Taft-Hartley to cripple organizing
- Full employment gave workers leverage
- Public credit to businesses required respecting collective bargaining

**Impact:** By 1950, unionization was the norm in manufacturing, construction, and transportation. Unions were setting wage standards across entire industries.

---

#### **Strikes: 470 vs. 180 per year**
Fewer strikes because there was less to strike *about*.

In our timeline, unions struck for:
- Wage increases (to keep pace with inflation)
- Job security (layoffs during credit contractions)
- Benefits (employers cut costs)

In the alternate timeline:
- Wages rose automatically with productivity (less need to fight)
- Full employment meant layoffs were rare
- Benefits were universal (healthcare public, pensions secure)

**Impact:** Labor peace. Strikes still happened—democracy requires friction—but the desperation strikes of our timeline were absent.

### Conclusion: The Numbers Don't Lie

This table is not propaganda. These are the mathematical consequences of removing private credit creation from the economy.

Every percentage point, every dollar, every ratio tells the same story:

**When credit serves the public, prosperity is broadly shared.**  
**When credit serves private profit, wealth concentrates.**

The alternate timeline was not utopia. People still worked. Inequality still existed. Conflicts still arose.

But the *trendlines* were different. Wealth was spreading, not concentrating. Wages were rising, not stagnating. Debt was falling, not exploding. Homeownership was expanding, not gatekept.

The economic table is the autopsy of the world we lost.

And the blueprint of the world we can still build.
